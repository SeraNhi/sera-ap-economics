Aggregate Supply (AS)

Learn how businesses determine the total quantity of goods and services supplied in an economy and how changes in costs, productivity, and resources influence economic output.

Topic Introduction

While Aggregate Demand focuses on spending throughout the economy, Aggregate Supply focuses on production. Businesses decide how much output they are willing and able to produce at different price levels. These production decisions determine the economy's total supply of goods and services.

Aggregate Supply is one of the most frequently tested concepts in AP Macroeconomics because it helps explain inflation, unemployment, economic growth, and supply-side shocks.

Understanding Aggregate Supply is essential for interpreting AD-AS graphs and analyzing government policies.

What Is Aggregate Supply?

Aggregate Supply (AS) represents the total quantity of final goods and services that firms are willing and able to produce at various overall price levels during a given period.

Aggregate Supply examines production across the entire economy rather than the supply of a single good.

Economists divide Aggregate Supply into two important concepts: Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS) and Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS).

Short-Run Aggregate Supply (SRAS)

Short-Run Aggregate Supply shows the relationship between the overall price level and the quantity of output firms are willing to produce when some production costs remain fixed.

In the short run, wages and certain input costs may not adjust immediately, allowing firms to respond to changing prices by increasing or decreasing production.

The SRAS curve slopes upward from left to right.

Long-Run Aggregate Supply (LRAS)

Long-Run Aggregate Supply represents the economy's maximum sustainable level of output when all resources are fully employed.

In the long run, wages and prices are flexible, so the economy naturally moves toward its full-employment level of production.

LRAS reflects productive capacity rather than current spending conditions.

Why Does SRAS Slope Upward?

The SRAS curve slopes upward because firms are willing to increase production when output prices rise faster than some of their production costs.

Higher prices often increase profits, encouraging firms to hire additional workers and expand output.

Key Reason

  • Some wages and input prices adjust slowly in the short run.
  • Higher product prices increase profitability.
  • Businesses expand production.

Why Is LRAS Vertical?

The LRAS curve is vertical because long-run output depends on available resources, technology, and productivity rather than the price level.

Even if prices rise, the economy cannot permanently produce beyond its full-employment capacity.

In the long run, economic growth requires increases in productive capacity rather than simply higher prices.

Input Prices

Input prices are among the most important determinants of Aggregate Supply.

Examples of Inputs

  • Labor.
  • Raw materials.
  • Energy.
  • Transportation.
  • Machinery and equipment.

When input prices rise, production becomes more expensive and SRAS shifts left.

When input prices fall, production becomes less expensive and SRAS shifts right.

Productivity

Productivity measures how efficiently resources are transformed into output.

Higher productivity allows firms to produce more goods and services using the same amount of resources.

Productivity improvements shift SRAS and LRAS to the right because production becomes more efficient.

Sources of Productivity Growth

  • Worker training.
  • Technological innovation.
  • Better management practices.
  • Improved infrastructure.

Resource Costs

Resource costs include wages, rent, interest, and the cost of raw materials.

Rising resource costs increase production expenses and reduce profitability.

As costs increase, firms may reduce production, causing SRAS to shift left.

Business Taxes and Subsidies

Business Taxes

Higher business taxes increase production costs.

As costs rise, firms produce less at each price level, causing SRAS to shift left.

Subsidies

Subsidies are government payments that reduce production costs.

Lower costs encourage production and shift SRAS to the right.

Supply Shocks

A supply shock is an unexpected event that significantly changes production costs or productive capacity.

Negative Supply Shocks

  • Oil price spikes.
  • Natural disasters.
  • Major wars.
  • Supply chain disruptions.

Negative shocks typically shift SRAS left.

Positive Supply Shocks

  • Technological breakthroughs.
  • Falling energy prices.
  • Productivity improvements.

Positive shocks generally shift SRAS right.

Stagflation

Stagflation is one of the most important AP Macroeconomics concepts.

Stagflation occurs when an economy experiences:

  • High inflation.
  • High unemployment.
  • Slow economic growth.

Stagflation is often caused by a negative supply shock that shifts SRAS left.

As SRAS decreases, output falls while the price level rises, creating the unusual combination of inflation and unemployment.

SRAS vs. LRAS Shifts

SRAS Shifts

SRAS shifts are usually caused by changes in production costs.

  • Input prices.
  • Taxes.
  • Subsidies.
  • Temporary supply shocks.

LRAS Shifts

LRAS shifts occur when productive capacity changes.

  • More capital.
  • Better technology.
  • Higher productivity.
  • Growth in human capital.
  • Additional natural resources.

LRAS shifts represent long-run economic growth.

AD-AS Equilibrium

Macroeconomic equilibrium occurs where Aggregate Demand and Aggregate Supply intersect.

At equilibrium:

  • The quantity of output demanded equals output supplied.
  • The economy operates at a specific real GDP level.
  • A particular overall price level is established.

Changes in either AD or AS can move the economy away from its original equilibrium.

AP Graph Analysis

Rightward SRAS Shift

  • Real GDP increases.
  • Price level decreases.
  • Economic growth improves.

Leftward SRAS Shift

  • Real GDP decreases.
  • Price level increases.
  • Potential stagflation.

Rightward LRAS Shift

  • Long-run economic growth.
  • Higher productive capacity.
  • Greater potential output.

AP exam questions frequently ask students to identify which curve shifts and explain the resulting changes in output and prices.

AP Exam Tips

  • Know the difference between SRAS and LRAS.
  • Memorize major SRAS shifters.
  • Understand factors that shift LRAS.
  • Practice identifying supply shocks.
  • Know the causes of stagflation.
  • Master AD-AS graph analysis.
  • Remember that LRAS represents full-employment output.

Common Student Mistakes

  • Confusing Aggregate Supply with Aggregate Demand.
  • Assuming SRAS and LRAS respond the same way.
  • Forgetting that LRAS is vertical.
  • Mixing up productivity and price-level changes.
  • Misidentifying supply shocks.
  • Confusing stagflation with ordinary inflation.

Summary

Aggregate Supply measures the total quantity of goods and services firms are willing and able to produce. Short-Run Aggregate Supply responds to changing costs and prices, while Long-Run Aggregate Supply reflects the economy's productive capacity.

Changes in input prices, productivity, taxes, subsidies, and supply shocks influence Aggregate Supply and play a major role in determining inflation, unemployment, and economic growth.

Key Takeaways

  • Aggregate Supply measures economy-wide production.
  • SRAS slopes upward.
  • LRAS is vertical.
  • Input costs strongly influence SRAS.
  • Productivity improvements increase supply.
  • Taxes decrease supply while subsidies increase supply.
  • Supply shocks can significantly affect the economy.
  • Stagflation results from negative supply shocks.
  • LRAS shifts indicate long-run economic growth.
