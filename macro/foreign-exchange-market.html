Foreign Exchange Market

Understand how currencies are exchanged, what determines exchange rates, and how global currency movements affect trade, investment, and economic stability.

1. Topic Introduction

Every international transaction requires an exchange of currency. Whether buying imported goods, investing abroad, or traveling, currencies must be converted through the foreign exchange market.

Exchange rates play a crucial role in globalization because they determine how expensive or cheap a country’s goods are relative to others.

Changes in currency values can significantly impact exports, imports, inflation, and overall economic performance.

2. What is Foreign Exchange?

Foreign exchange refers to the system in which one currency is exchanged for another. This takes place in the foreign exchange market, a global decentralized marketplace.

Currency Exchange Basics

  • Currencies are traded in pairs (e.g., USD/EUR).
  • The exchange rate shows the value of one currency in terms of another.
  • Exchange rates fluctuate constantly due to supply and demand.

Foreign Exchange Market

The foreign exchange market is the largest financial market in the world, where banks, governments, firms, and individuals trade currencies.

3. Exchange Rates

An exchange rate is the price of one currency expressed in terms of another.

Appreciation

Appreciation occurs when a currency increases in value relative to another currency.

Depreciation

Depreciation occurs when a currency decreases in value relative to another currency.

Exchange rate movements directly affect international competitiveness.

4. Determinants of Exchange Rates

Several factors influence the value of a currency in the foreign exchange market.

  • Interest Rates: Higher interest rates attract foreign investment.
  • Inflation Rates: Lower inflation increases currency value.
  • Economic Growth: Strong economies attract capital inflows.
  • Trade Balances: Export-heavy economies increase currency demand.

5. Demand for Currency

Demand for a currency comes from foreign buyers who need it to purchase goods, services, or assets from that country.

Main Sources of Demand

  • Foreign demand for exports.
  • Foreign investment in domestic assets.
  • Tourism spending in the country.

6. Supply of Currency

Supply of a currency comes from domestic buyers who need foreign currency to purchase imports or invest abroad.

Main Sources of Supply

  • Imports of goods and services.
  • Investment in foreign countries.
  • Capital outflows.

7. Currency Appreciation

When a currency appreciates, it becomes stronger relative to other currencies.

Effects on Exports

Exports become more expensive for foreign buyers, which may reduce demand.

Effects on Imports

Imports become cheaper for domestic consumers, increasing import consumption.

8. Currency Depreciation

When a currency depreciates, it becomes weaker relative to other currencies.

Effects on Exports

Exports become cheaper for foreign buyers, increasing demand for domestic goods.

Effects on Imports

Imports become more expensive, reducing import consumption and potentially increasing domestic production.

9. Fixed vs Floating Exchange Rates

Floating Exchange Rate

In a floating system, exchange rates are determined by market forces of supply and demand.

Fixed Exchange Rate

In a fixed system, a government or central bank maintains the currency at a set value by intervening in the foreign exchange market.

Most major economies use floating exchange rates, while some developing countries use managed or fixed systems.

10. Effects on Trade Balance

Exchange rates strongly influence a country’s trade balance.

  • Stronger currency: imports increase, exports decrease.
  • Weaker currency: exports increase, imports decrease.

These changes affect trade surpluses and deficits over time.

11. Real-World Examples

US Dollar Strength

When the US dollar appreciates, imported goods become cheaper for US consumers but US exports become less competitive globally.

Vietnamese Dong Context

A relatively weaker currency can support export-driven industries by making goods more affordable internationally.

Euro Fluctuations

Changes in the euro affect trade across the European Union and global markets, influencing tourism and investment flows.

12. AP Exam Graph Analysis

Currency Market Graph

AP questions often show a foreign exchange graph with supply and demand curves for currency. Students must identify appreciation and depreciation.

Shifts in Currency Demand and Supply

  • Increase in exports → demand for currency increases.
  • Increase in imports → supply of currency increases.
  • Higher interest rates → demand increases due to capital inflows.

13. AP Exam Tips

  • Always label currency graphs correctly (USD vs foreign currency).
  • Know causes of appreciation vs depreciation.
  • Connect exports to currency demand.
  • Connect imports to currency supply.
  • Practice shifting curves in FX graphs.

14. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing appreciation with depreciation.
  • Reversing supply and demand in FX graphs.
  • Mixing up imports and exports effects.
  • Ignoring capital flows in exchange rate analysis.

15. Summary

The foreign exchange market determines the value of currencies and plays a central role in international trade and finance. Exchange rates are influenced by trade flows, investment flows, interest rates, and inflation.

16. Key Takeaways

  • Exchange rates determine currency value.
  • Appreciation strengthens a currency.
  • Depreciation weakens a currency.
  • Exports increase currency demand.
  • Imports increase currency supply.
  • Capital flows significantly impact exchange rates.
  • FX markets affect global trade and economic stability.
