Supply and Demand

Understand how markets determine prices, allocate resources, and respond to changes in consumer and producer behavior.

Topic Introduction

Supply and demand form the foundation of market economics. Nearly every market—from smartphones and coffee to labor and housing—operates according to the interaction of buyers and sellers.

Demand represents the behavior of consumers, while supply represents the behavior of producers. Together, they determine market prices and quantities.

Supply and demand is one of the most frequently tested topics in AP Economics and serves as a building block for many later units.

Understanding Demand

Demand refers to the quantity of a good or service consumers are willing and able to purchase at various prices during a given period of time.

According to the Law of Demand, as price increases, quantity demanded decreases. Conversely, as price decreases, quantity demanded increases.

This inverse relationship exists because consumers generally seek the greatest value for their money and because substitutes become more attractive when prices rise.

Demand Curve Characteristics

  • Downward sloping from left to right.
  • Shows an inverse relationship between price and quantity demanded.
  • Represents consumer behavior.
  • Assumes all other factors remain constant.

Determinants of Demand

Several factors can cause the entire demand curve to shift.

Determinant Effect on Demand
Income Higher income increases demand for normal goods.
Consumer Preferences Greater popularity increases demand.
Population Larger populations increase demand.
Expectations Future price expectations affect current demand.
Prices of Related Goods Substitutes and complements influence demand.

Remember: a change in price causes movement along the demand curve, while changes in these determinants cause the entire curve to shift.

Understanding Supply

Supply refers to the quantity of a good or service producers are willing and able to offer for sale at various prices.

According to the Law of Supply, as price increases, quantity supplied increases. As price decreases, quantity supplied decreases.

Higher prices create stronger incentives for producers to increase production because profits become more attractive.

Supply Curve Characteristics

  • Upward sloping from left to right.
  • Shows a direct relationship between price and quantity supplied.
  • Represents producer behavior.
  • Reflects incentives for profit.

Determinants of Supply

Several factors can shift the entire supply curve.

Determinant Effect on Supply
Input Costs Higher costs decrease supply.
Technology Improved technology increases supply.
Taxes and Regulations Often reduce supply.
Number of Sellers More sellers increase supply.
Producer Expectations Future expectations influence current supply.

Just like demand, a price change causes movement along the supply curve rather than a shift.

Market Equilibrium

Equilibrium occurs when quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

At equilibrium:

  • Buyers and sellers agree on price.
  • No shortage exists.
  • No surplus exists.
  • The market clears efficiently.

The equilibrium price is often called the market-clearing price. The equilibrium quantity is the amount bought and sold in the market.

Competitive markets naturally move toward equilibrium through price adjustments.

Shortages

A shortage occurs when quantity demanded exceeds quantity supplied.

Shortages typically occur when prices are set below equilibrium.

During a shortage:

  • Consumers compete for limited goods.
  • Prices tend to rise.
  • Producers increase output.
  • The market moves toward equilibrium.

Example: During major product launches, demand may initially exceed supply, creating shortages and long waiting lists.

Surpluses

A surplus occurs when quantity supplied exceeds quantity demanded.

Surpluses generally occur when prices are above equilibrium.

During a surplus:

  • Unsold inventory accumulates.
  • Businesses reduce prices.
  • Producers cut output.
  • The market moves toward equilibrium.

Retail stores often hold sales when excess inventory creates surpluses.

Price Signals

Prices serve as signals in market economies.

Rising prices communicate information about scarcity and increased demand. Falling prices communicate information about surpluses or reduced demand.

These signals help coordinate economic activity without requiring central planning.

Producers use prices to determine what to produce, while consumers use prices to make purchasing decisions.

Shifts vs. Movements Along Curves

Change Result
Change in Price Movement Along the Curve
Change in Income Demand Shift
Change in Tastes Demand Shift
Change in Technology Supply Shift
Change in Input Costs Supply Shift

One of the most common AP exam mistakes is confusing movement along a curve with a shift of the entire curve.

Real World Examples

Coffee Market

A drought that damages coffee crops reduces supply. Prices rise and quantity sold decreases.

Smartphones

A highly anticipated smartphone release increases demand, leading to higher prices and possible shortages.

Gasoline

Rising oil prices increase production costs, reducing supply and causing gasoline prices to increase.

Streaming Services

Increased popularity of streaming platforms shifts demand to the right as more consumers subscribe.

AP Exam Tips

  • Know every determinant of supply and demand.
  • Memorize the difference between shifts and movements.
  • Always identify whether supply, demand, or both changed.
  • Practice predicting equilibrium changes.
  • Understand shortages and surpluses thoroughly.
  • Use correct graph labels on exams.

Common Student Mistakes

  • Confusing quantity demanded with demand.
  • Confusing quantity supplied with supply.
  • Mixing up shortages and surpluses.
  • Forgetting that price changes cause movements along curves.
  • Drawing supply and demand curves incorrectly.

Summary

Supply and demand determine how markets allocate resources. Demand reflects consumer behavior, while supply reflects producer behavior. Their interaction creates equilibrium prices and quantities.

Changes in determinants shift curves, while changes in price cause movements along curves. Understanding these relationships is essential for success in AP Economics.

Key Takeaways

  • Demand slopes downward.
  • Supply slopes upward.
  • Equilibrium occurs where supply equals demand.
  • Shortages push prices upward.
  • Surpluses push prices downward.
  • Price changes cause movements along curves.
  • Determinant changes shift curves.
  • Markets use prices as signals.
