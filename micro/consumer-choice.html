Consumer Choice

How consumers make rational decisions to maximize satisfaction within limited budgets.

1. Topic Introduction

Consumer choice theory explains how individuals decide what to buy when they have limited income and unlimited wants. Because resources are scarce, consumers must make rational decisions about how to allocate their money.

Every purchase involves trade-offs. Choosing one good means giving up another. The goal is to maximize satisfaction, also known as utility.

Real-World Example

A student in Ho Chi Minh City has limited money and must choose between:

  • Bubble tea
  • Saving for a new phone
  • Buying street food

This is consumer choice in action.

2. What is Utility?

Utility is the satisfaction or happiness a consumer receives from consuming goods and services.

Economists assume consumers try to maximize total utility given their budget constraints.

Example

  • Eating pizza gives satisfaction
  • Watching a movie gives entertainment value

3. Marginal Utility

Marginal utility is the additional satisfaction gained from consuming one more unit of a good.

Example

  • First coffee → high satisfaction
  • Second coffee → less satisfaction
  • Third coffee → even less satisfaction

4. Law of Diminishing Marginal Utility

As more units of a good are consumed, the additional satisfaction from each new unit decreases.

Real-World Example

  • 1st mango → very enjoyable
  • 2nd mango → still good
  • 3rd mango → less enjoyable
  • 4th mango → no desire

5. Budget Constraint

Consumers have limited income, which restricts the combinations of goods they can purchase.

Example

A student with 100,000 VND must choose between:

  • Milk tea
  • Snacks
  • Entertainment

Not all options can be purchased at the same time.

6. Utility Maximization Rule

Consumers maximize satisfaction by allocating their spending so that:

Marginal Utility per Dollar is equal across all goods.

Meaning

Consumers shift spending until each dollar spent gives equal satisfaction.

7. Consumer Optimization

The optimal consumption point occurs when a consumer gets the highest possible utility within their budget constraint.

Example

  • Balancing spending between food and entertainment
  • Choosing between saving and consumption

8. Real-World Examples

Student Spending

A student allocates allowance between food, drinks, and transport to maximize satisfaction.

Smartphone Purchase

Consumers choose between upgrading phone or spending on accessories based on value.

Fast Food Decisions

Customers choose combo meals that give the highest satisfaction per dollar.

9. AP Exam Focus

  • Calculate marginal utility
  • Find optimal consumption point
  • Use MU per dollar rule
  • Interpret utility tables

10. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing total utility with marginal utility
  • Ignoring budget constraints
  • Thinking consumers maximize total happiness instead of efficiency

11. Summary

Consumers make rational decisions by comparing marginal utility and allocating limited income to maximize total satisfaction.

12. Key Takeaways

  • Utility = satisfaction
  • Marginal utility decreases over time
  • Consumers face budget constraints
  • Optimal choice maximizes utility per dollar
