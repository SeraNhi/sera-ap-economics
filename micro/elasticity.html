Elasticity

Measuring how sensitive consumers and producers are to changes in price and income.

1. Topic Introduction

Elasticity measures how responsive buyers and sellers are when prices or other economic factors change. Not all goods respond the same way — some are highly sensitive, while others barely change in demand or supply.

Elasticity is crucial for understanding business decisions, tax policies, and consumer behavior. It helps explain how markets react when conditions change.

Real-World Examples

  • If Netflix raises its subscription price slightly, many users cancel → elastic demand
  • If gas prices increase, people still continue buying fuel → inelastic demand

2. Price Elasticity of Demand (PED)

Price Elasticity of Demand (PED) measures how much quantity demanded changes when price changes.

It is based on percentage changes in quantity demanded and price.

Interpretation

  • Elastic (PED > 1): Quantity changes a lot when price changes
  • Inelastic (PED < 1): Quantity changes very little
  • Unit elastic (PED = 1): Proportional change

Real-World Examples

  • Luxury handbags → elastic demand (price increase reduces purchases)
  • Medicine → inelastic demand (people need it regardless of price)

3. Elastic vs Inelastic Demand

  • Elastic: Fast food, luxury goods, entertainment
  • Inelastic: Gasoline, electricity, medicine
  • Unit elastic: Rare cases where proportional changes match

Real-World Example

  • 🍔 Fast food → elastic (many substitutes available)
  • ⛽ Gasoline → inelastic (few alternatives in short run)

4. Determinants of Elasticity

  • Availability of substitutes
  • Necessity vs luxury
  • Time period (short run vs long run)
  • Share of income

Real-World Examples

  • Electricity → inelastic (no close substitutes)
  • Coffee brands → elastic (many alternatives)

5. Total Revenue Test

Total Revenue = Price × Quantity

Elasticity determines how revenue changes when price changes.

Real-World Example

  • If concert ticket prices increase:
    • Elastic demand → fewer buyers → revenue falls
    • Inelastic demand → revenue rises

6. Price Elasticity of Supply (PES)

PES measures how quickly producers adjust output when prices change.

Real-World Examples

  • Farm crops → inelastic supply (takes time to grow)
  • Manufactured goods → more elastic supply (can scale production faster)

7. Elasticity Over Time

Elasticity increases over time because consumers and producers have more options to adjust.

Example: Gas Prices

  • Short run → people still buy fuel (inelastic)
  • Long run → people switch to electric vehicles (more elastic)

8. Real-World Integrated Examples

Starbucks Coffee

Small price increase → most customers still buy → inelastic demand

Airplane Tickets

Price increase → fewer travelers → elastic demand

Salt

Price changes have almost no effect → perfectly inelastic behavior

9. AP Exam Graph Analysis

  • Steeper demand curve → inelastic
  • Flatter demand curve → elastic
  • Total revenue rectangles used in questions

Common Tasks

  • Identify elasticity from graphs
  • Predict revenue changes
  • Compare goods based on elasticity

10. AP Exam Tips

  • Always check substitutes first
  • Use total revenue rule correctly
  • Do NOT confuse slope with elasticity

11. Common Mistakes

  • Thinking steep curve = elastic (WRONG)
  • Confusing shift vs elasticity
  • Ignoring time effects

12. Summary

Elasticity measures responsiveness of buyers and sellers. It is essential for pricing decisions, taxation, and market analysis.

13. Key Takeaways

  • Elastic = highly responsive
  • Inelastic = not very responsive
  • Revenue depends on elasticity
  • Time increases elasticity
