Monopoly

Understanding markets with a single seller, high barriers to entry, and price-making power.

1. Topic Introduction

A monopoly is a market structure where a single firm is the only seller of a good or service with no close substitutes. This gives the firm significant control over price and output.

Real-World Example

Electricity supply in many regions is controlled by one company:

  • Only one provider exists
  • Consumers have no alternatives
  • The firm sets the price

2. Characteristics of Monopoly

  • Single seller in the market
  • No close substitutes
  • High barriers to entry
  • Price maker (not price taker)
  • Controls both output and price

Real-World Examples

  • Water supply systems
  • Postal services in some countries
  • Pharmaceutical patents

3. Sources of Monopoly Power (Barriers to Entry)

  • Legal barriers (patents, licenses)
  • Economies of scale (natural monopoly)
  • Control of key resources
  • Network effects (platform dominance)

Real-World Examples

  • Electricity grids → natural monopoly
  • Pharmaceutical companies → legal monopoly through patents
  • Tech platforms → strong network effects

4. Demand Curve for a Monopoly

A monopoly faces the entire market demand curve, which is downward sloping. Unlike competitive firms, monopolies must lower price to sell more output.

Real-World Example

If electricity prices increase:

  • Consumers still need electricity
  • But may reduce usage slightly

5. Revenue Concepts

  • Total Revenue = Price × Quantity
  • Marginal Revenue declines faster than demand

Real-World Example

When a monopoly lowers price to sell more units, each additional unit adds less revenue than the previous one.

6. Profit Maximization Rule

Monopolists maximize profit where marginal revenue equals marginal cost.

MR = MC

Real-World Example

An electricity company increases production until the cost of producing one more unit equals the revenue it generates.

7. Price and Output in Monopoly

  • Monopolists charge higher prices
  • They produce less output than competitive markets

Real-World Example

If only one internet provider exists in an area:

  • Prices tend to be higher
  • Service options may be limited

8. Economic Profit in Monopoly

  • Monopolies can earn long-run economic profit
  • High barriers prevent entry of competitors

Real-World Example

Pharmaceutical companies with patents can earn high profits for many years.

9. Efficiency in Monopoly

  • Not productively efficient
  • Not allocatively efficient
  • Creates deadweight loss

Real-World Example

Electricity monopolies often charge higher prices, reducing consumer access and creating inefficiency.

10. Real-World Integrated Examples

Electricity Utility

  • Single provider
  • High infrastructure costs
  • Often regulated by government

Pharmaceutical Patents

  • Legal monopoly over drugs
  • High prices during patent period

Tech Platforms

  • Strong network effects
  • High switching costs for users

11. AP Exam Graph Analysis

  • Market demand curve
  • Marginal revenue curve below demand
  • Marginal cost curve
  • Profit rectangle
  • Deadweight loss triangle

Common AP Tasks

  • Identify profit levels
  • Show inefficiency (deadweight loss)
  • Compare monopoly vs competition outcomes

12. AP Exam Tips

  • MR is always below demand curve
  • Monopoly = price maker
  • Profit = (P - ATC) × Q
  • Always expect deadweight loss

13. Common Mistakes

  • Thinking monopoly cannot earn profit
  • Confusing MR with demand curve
  • Assuming monopolies are efficient

14. Summary

A monopoly is a market with a single seller that controls price and output, leading to higher prices, lower output, and inefficiency.

15. Key Takeaways

  • Monopoly = single seller
  • Price maker, not price taker
  • MR < demand curve
  • Creates deadweight loss
