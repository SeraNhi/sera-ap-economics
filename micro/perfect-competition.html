Perfect Competition

Understanding how firms behave in highly competitive markets where no one controls price.

1. Topic Introduction

Perfect competition is a market structure where many firms sell identical products and no single firm has control over price. All firms are price takers, meaning they must accept the market price determined by supply and demand.

Real-World Example

Rice farmers in Vietnam operate in a highly competitive market:

  • Thousands of farmers sell rice
  • All rice is similar in quality
  • No farmer can influence market price

They must accept the market price.

2. Characteristics of Perfect Competition

  • Many buyers and sellers
  • Identical products (homogeneous goods)
  • Free entry and exit of firms
  • Perfect information available to all participants
  • Firms are price takers

Real-World Examples

  • Agricultural markets (rice, wheat, vegetables)
  • Local street markets with identical goods

3. Demand Curve for a Firm

In perfect competition, the firm faces a horizontal demand curve at the market price.

Example

If the market price of rice is $5 per kg:

  • The farmer cannot charge $6 (buyers will not buy)
  • The farmer cannot charge $4 (loses revenue)
  • Must accept $5

4. Revenue Concepts

  • Total Revenue (TR): Price × Quantity
  • Marginal Revenue (MR): Equals price in perfect competition

Real-World Example

Each additional unit of rice sold adds exactly the market price to total revenue.

5. Profit Maximization Rule

Firms maximize profit where:

MR = MC

Real-World Example

A farmer increases production until the cost of producing one more unit of rice equals the price he receives.

6. Short-Run Profit or Loss

  • Economic profit may exist in the short run
  • Normal profit means breaking even
  • Firms may continue operating even with losses

Real-World Example

  • High demand season → coffee farmers earn profit
  • Drought season → losses but farmers still produce

7. Long-Run Equilibrium

In the long run, economic profit is driven to zero due to entry and exit of firms.

Real-World Example

  • Profitable rice farming → more farmers enter → price falls
  • Unprofitable market → farmers exit → price rises

8. Efficiency in Perfect Competition

  • Productive Efficiency: ATC is at minimum
  • Allocative Efficiency: P = MC

Real-World Example

Agricultural markets tend to produce goods at the lowest possible cost in the long run.

9. Real-World Integrated Examples

Rice Farming (Vietnam)

  • Many small farmers
  • Identical products
  • Market determines price

Vegetable Markets

  • Price competition only
  • No branding power

Street Fruit Vendors

  • Similar products
  • Buyers choose lowest price

10. AP Exam Graph Analysis

  • Horizontal demand curve (MR = P)
  • MC curve
  • ATC curve
  • Profit and loss areas

Common Tasks

  • Identify profit or loss situations
  • Find profit-maximizing output
  • Compare short run vs long run outcomes

11. AP Exam Tips

  • Perfect competition = price taker ALWAYS
  • MR always equals price
  • Long run = zero economic profit

12. Common Mistakes

  • Thinking firms can set price (incorrect)
  • Confusing monopoly with perfect competition
  • Forgetting MR = P rule

13. Summary

Perfect competition is a market structure where many firms compete, no firm has pricing power, and profits are eliminated in the long run.

14. Key Takeaways

  • Firms are price takers
  • MR = P in perfect competition
  • Long-run profit = zero
  • Efficiency is highest in this market
