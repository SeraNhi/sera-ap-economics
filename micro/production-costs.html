Production & Costs

How firms make production decisions by analyzing costs, efficiency, and profit maximization.

1. Topic Introduction

Firms exist to maximize profit, and production decisions are at the core of this goal. To survive in competitive markets, businesses must carefully analyze their costs and efficiency levels.

Production decisions determine how much a firm should produce, while cost analysis determines whether production is profitable.

Real-World Example

A milk tea shop in Ho Chi Minh City must balance ingredient costs and selling prices. If ingredient prices rise too much, profit decreases, forcing the business to adjust production.

2. Types of Costs

  • Fixed Costs: Costs that do not change with output (rent, machinery)
  • Variable Costs: Costs that change with output (labor, ingredients)
  • Total Cost: Fixed cost + variable cost

Real-World Examples

  • Rent → fixed cost (paid every month regardless of output)
  • Workers’ wages → variable cost (depends on production level)
  • Ingredients → variable cost (increase with more production)

3. Marginal Cost (MC)

Marginal cost is the cost of producing one additional unit of output.

Real-World Example

If a coffee shop produces one more cup:

  • If MC is low → production increases
  • If MC rises → production becomes less efficient

4. Average Costs

  • Average Total Cost (ATC): total cost per unit
  • Average Fixed Cost (AFC): fixed cost per unit
  • Average Variable Cost (AVC): variable cost per unit

Real-World Example

A bakery spreads fixed rent over more bread production, reducing average cost per unit. However, as output increases, costs may change due to efficiency effects.

5. Production in the Short Run

In the short run, at least one input is fixed (such as capital or factory size).

Real-World Example

  • Adding workers increases output at first
  • Too many workers leads to overcrowding and inefficiency

6. Law of Diminishing Marginal Returns

As more units of a variable input are added, the additional output eventually decreases.

Real-World Example (Kitchen Scenario)

  • 1 chef → slow production
  • 2 chefs → efficient production
  • 5 chefs → overcrowded kitchen, lower efficiency

7. Profit Maximization Rule

Firms maximize profit where marginal revenue equals marginal cost.

MR = MC

Real-World Example

A phone manufacturer continues producing units until the cost of producing one more phone equals the revenue earned from it.

8. Real-World Integrated Examples

Starbucks

  • Fixed costs: rent, machines
  • Variable costs: coffee beans, labor
  • Expansion improves efficiency through economies of scale

Clothing Factory

  • Initial workers increase output
  • Too many workers reduce productivity

Street Food Vendor

  • Very low fixed cost
  • Profit depends heavily on ingredient price changes

9. AP Exam Graph Analysis

  • Marginal Cost (MC) curve
  • Average Total Cost (ATC) curve (U-shaped)
  • Average Variable Cost (AVC) curve
  • Profit-maximizing output where MR = MC

Common AP Tasks

  • Identify profit-maximizing quantity
  • Interpret cost curve relationships
  • Analyze efficiency changes

10. AP Exam Tips

  • Always apply MR = MC rule
  • MC intersects ATC at its lowest point
  • Diminishing returns causes MC to rise

11. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing fixed and variable costs
  • Thinking profit maximization is total revenue
  • Misreading cost curve shapes

12. Summary

Firms make production decisions by analyzing costs and efficiency. Profit is maximized when marginal revenue equals marginal cost.

13. Key Takeaways

  • Costs determine production decisions
  • MC guides firm behavior
  • Diminishing returns increases cost
  • Profit max occurs at MR = MC
