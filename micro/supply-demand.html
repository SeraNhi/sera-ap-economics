Supply & Demand

The core engine of microeconomics: how markets allocate resources through price signals.

1. Topic Introduction

Markets are systems where buyers and sellers interact to exchange goods and services. The forces of supply and demand determine prices, quantities, and resource allocation in almost every economy.

Prices act as signals. When prices rise or fall, they communicate information about scarcity and value, guiding both consumers and producers in their decisions.

Supply and demand is the foundation of microeconomics because nearly every market outcome depends on it.

Real-World Example

If coffee prices rise in Ho Chi Minh City, cafés may:

  • Increase prices for customers
  • Reduce quantity demanded
  • Search for cheaper suppliers

This is supply and demand in action.

2. What is Demand?

Demand refers to the willingness and ability of consumers to buy a good or service at different prices.

The law of demand states that as price increases, quantity demanded decreases, and vice versa.

Real-World Examples

  • When iPhone prices drop, more students buy them.
  • When movie ticket prices increase, fewer people attend cinemas.

This inverse relationship is shown by a downward-sloping demand curve.

3. What is Supply?

Supply refers to the willingness and ability of producers to sell a good or service at different prices.

The law of supply states that as price increases, quantity supplied increases.

Real-World Examples

  • If rice prices increase, farmers in Vietnam grow more rice.
  • If labor costs rise, factories may reduce production.

This relationship creates an upward-sloping supply curve.

4. Market Equilibrium

Market equilibrium occurs where quantity demanded equals quantity supplied.

At this point, there is no shortage or surplus, and the market is stable.

Real-World Example: Street Food Market

  • If price is too high → surplus (unsold food)
  • If price is too low → shortage (long lines, empty stalls)
  • At equilibrium → smooth and stable transactions

5. Shifts in Demand

Demand shifts when non-price factors change.

Determinants of Demand

  • Income
  • Tastes and preferences
  • Population size
  • Expectations
  • Prices of substitutes

Real-World Examples

  • Trending TikTok boba tea → demand increases
  • Lower income → demand for luxury goods decreases

6. Shifts in Supply

Supply shifts when production conditions change.

Determinants of Supply

  • Input costs
  • Technology
  • Taxes and subsidies
  • Number of sellers
  • Weather conditions

Real-World Examples

  • Drought reduces coffee supply → prices rise
  • New machines increase smartphone production

7. Movement vs Shift

Understanding the difference between movement along a curve and a shift of the curve is essential for AP exams.

  • Movement: caused by a change in price
  • Shift: caused by changes in determinants

Real-World Example

  • Price of coffee increases → movement along demand curve
  • Health trend increases tea popularity → demand shifts right

8. Surplus & Shortage Analysis

Markets naturally adjust using price signals.

Real-World Examples

  • Too many sneakers → prices drop (surplus)
  • Limited PS5 consoles → prices rise or scalpers appear (shortage)

Prices move toward equilibrium over time.

9. Real-World Integrated Example

Street Milk Tea Market (Vietnam)

  • Summer increases demand for cold drinks
  • Ingredient costs increase, limiting supply

Result:

  • Prices increase
  • New sellers enter the market
  • Market adjusts toward a new equilibrium

10. AP Exam Focus

  • Identify shifts in demand and supply
  • Draw and interpret equilibrium changes
  • Distinguish surplus vs shortage
  • Analyze determinants correctly

11. Common Mistakes

  • Confusing shift vs movement
  • Mixing determinants of supply and demand
  • Thinking price changes shift curves

12. Summary

Supply and demand determine prices in all markets. Changes in either side shift equilibrium and influence real-world economic outcomes.

13. Key Takeaways

  • Supply and demand determine market prices
  • Shifts change equilibrium
  • Movements are caused only by price changes
  • Markets continuously adjust to new conditions
